👻 What is Pepper’s Ghost?

✨ A 19th-century illusion that makes objects appear as **ghostly projections**.

  • Invented by **John Henry Pepper** in 1862.
  • Uses a **transparent surface** at an angle to reflect hidden objects.
  • Originally used in **theater**—now seen in **holograms & live events**!

🔮 The tech behind **Tupac at Coachella & Disneys Haunted Mansion**!

📜 The History of Pepper's Ghost

Invented by John Henry Pepper in 1862

First used in theaters for ghostly illusions 👻

Now used in concerts (Tupac, Hatsune Miku) 🎤

"John Henry Pepper, a British scientist and lecturer, is best known for his demonstration of the Pepper's Ghost effect in 1862 at the Royal Polytechnic Institution in London. During a lecture, he used a transparent surface at a 45-degree angle to reflect an image from a hidden light source, creating the illusion of a ghostly figure that appeared to float in midair. This groundbreaking technique amazed audiences and became a defining moment in the history of theatrical illusions."
  • Classic Illusion with Modern Impact
    The Haunted Mansion ride uses the Pepper’s Ghost technique to create lifelike ghostly apparitions — dancing spirits, floating heads, and more — by reflecting hidden animatronics off angled glass. Opened in 1969.
  • Timeless Theatrical Trick
    Dating back to the 1800s, Pepper’s Ghost relies on lighting, mirrors, and transparent surfaces — not true holograms, but eerily convincing.
  • Immersive Storytelling
    Disney's implementation elevated the effect, blending art, engineering, and narrative to create a seamless ghostly experience.
  • Legacy Lives On
    This same illusion technique stunned audiences in 2012 when Tupac “reappeared” at Coachella — showcasing how a 19th-century illusion became a 21st-century tech phenomenon.
  • From Theme Parks to Stages
    Pepper’s Ghost bridges nostalgic stagecraft and futuristic performance, blurring the lines between memory, magic, and media.
In 2012, Tupac Shakur’s hologram stunned the world at Coachella. This performance, over 15 years after his death, used the Pepper's Ghost technique to project a holographic Tupac rapping alongside live performers like Dr. Dre and Snoop Dogg. It was a game-changer, blending cutting-edge technology with the emotional depth of live performance. This was the first widely publicized use of holography in music, opening up a new realm of possibilities for posthumous performances. The success of Tupac's hologram sparked conversations about the potential of holography in the entertainment industry, influencing the way artists could be remembered and experienced by fans.
Hatsune Miku: Speaking Note Following Tupac’s hologram, we saw a significant leap with the rise of Hatsune Miku, a fully virtual pop star from Japan. Unlike Tupac, Miku’s performances aren’t based on a real person, but instead, she’s an entirely digital creation. Her holographic concerts are held worldwide, where thousands of fans gather to see her sing and dance, projected live in 3D. Miku’s concerts are a prime example of how holography can create immersive, interactive experiences that blur the lines between digital and physical realms. What’s more, Hatsune Miku represents the future of virtual performers—combining technology and entertainment in new ways that push the boundaries of what’s possible in live performance today.

🖥️ How the Code Works


import { PeppersGhostEffect } from 'three/addons/effects/PeppersGhostEffect.js';

const effect = new PeppersGhostEffect(renderer);
effect.setSize(window.innerWidth, window.innerHeight);
effect.cameraDistance = 5;

✨ Uses a custom shader for a floating holographic effect.

"This part of the code brings the Pepper's Ghost illusion to life using a custom shader from Three.js, a popular JavaScript library for creating 3D graphics in the browser. The shader simulates the floating holographic effect, while adjusting the size and camera distance ensures a seamless, realistic sense of depth."
